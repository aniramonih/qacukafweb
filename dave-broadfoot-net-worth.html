Dave Broadfoot Full Overview and Wiki

Early Life and Career Beginnings

Born on December 5, 1925, in North Vancouver, British Columbia, Broadfoot’s early life remains somewhat shrouded in mystery, with little known about his parents or siblings.

His foray into the world of entertainment began after serving in the Canadian Merchant Navy from 1943 to 1947.

Post-war, he found his calling in comedy, starting in community theatre in Vancouver before moving to Toronto in 1952.

Rise to Fame

In Toronto, Broadfoot’s career took off. He became a staple in stage revues like Spring Thaw and The Big Review, showcasing his knack for satire and character comedy.

His move to television was marked by appearances on CBC Television shows like The Big Revue, The Wayne and Shuster Show, and Comedy Café. His unique style, blending traditional stand-up with character creation, resonated with audiences nationwide.

Royal Canadian Air Farce and Character Comedy

His most notable stint was with the Royal Canadian Air Farce, where he was a member from 1973 to 1993.

Here, he created memorable characters like Big Bobby Clobber, a not-so-sharp hockey player, and Sgt. Renfrew of the RCMP, a character he performed for the RCMP on numerous occasions, even receiving honorary promotions.

Awards and Honors

His contributions to Canadian comedy didn’t go unnoticed. Broadfoot was an Officer of the Order of Canada and received the Governor General’s Performing Arts Award for Lifetime Artistic Achievement.

He won Gemini Awards for his comedy specials Old Enough To Say What I Want and Old Dog, New Tricks.

Contributions Beyond Comedy

Apart from his performances, Broadfoot was also a voice actor in animated Christmas specials and made a guest appearance in the TV series Puppets Who Kill.

His autobiography, Old Enough to Say What I Want, offers a glimpse into the life and mind of this comedic genius.

Legacy and Influence

Broadfoot’s influence extends beyond his lifetime. The Canadian Comedy Awards established the Dave Broadfoot Award for Special Achievement, ensuring his legacy continues to inspire future generations.

His style, characterized by satire and character-driven humor, has left an indelible mark on Canadian comedy.

Personal Life and Passing

He was married twice, first to Jean Templeton from 1959 to 1962, and then to Diane Simard from 1967 until his passing in 2016.

Broadfoot’s second marriage was a testament to enduring companionship and support. He was also a devoted father to his daughter, Valérie.

He passed away on November 1, 2016, at the age of 90, leaving behind a legacy as a Canadian comedy icon and a trailblazer in the entertainment industry.
