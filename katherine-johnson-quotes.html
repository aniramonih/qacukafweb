Katherine Johnson’s Early Life and Education

Katherine Johnson, originally known as Katherine Coleman, was born and raised in White Sulphur Springs, West Virginia. Despite facing limited opportunities for African American children in the segregated South, Johnson’s parents recognized her exceptional mathematical talents and encouraged her education. She graduated from high school at the age of 14 and went on to attend West Virginia State College, majoring in mathematics and French. Despite the challenges she faced as an African American woman pursuing a career in academia, Johnson remained determined and excelled in her studies.

Throughout her early life and education, Katherine Johnson’s passion for mathematics and her drive to overcome barriers propelled her forward. Her exceptional talent and dedication allowed her to shine brightly, even in the face of adversity. Johnson’s pursuit of knowledge and love for mathematics would eventually lead her to groundbreaking work at NASA, where she would make significant contributions to the field of space exploration.

Katherine Johnson’s Work at NASA

In 1953, Katherine Johnson joined the National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics’ (NACA) West Area Computing Unit, where she became one of the “human computers” – a group of African American women who manually performed complex calculations. Johnson’s exceptional talent and skills quickly gained recognition, and she played a crucial role in calculating trajectories, flight paths, and launch windows for various missions.

Her expertise in analytical geometry and celestial mechanics made her an invaluable asset to the Space Task Group, responsible for sending the first American astronauts into space. Notably, Johnson’s calculations were instrumental in verifying the safety of astronaut John Glenn’s orbital trajectory during the Mercury program in 1962.
